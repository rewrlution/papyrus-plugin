# Skill Design

> Covers the full skill lineup, what each skill is for, and how they differ from each other.

---

## The Five Skills

| Skill | Audience | Tone | Job |
|---|---|---|---|
| `standup` | Your team | Neutral | Communicate what you did yesterday |
| `coach` | Yourself | Honest / reflective | Understand your own performance over a time range |
| `resume` | Hiring managers | Strategic | Achievement bullets for job applications |
| `promote` | Manager / promo committee | Strategic | Make the case for promotion |
| `interview` | Interviewers | Strategic / polished | Prepare STAR stories for interviews |

All five read from the same journal data. What differs is the audience, the time range, and the intent.

---

## The honest / strategic split

`coach` is the only skill that's genuinely for the user — not for an audience. It gives an honest reflection: what the journals actually show, where the gaps are, where the user is strong. It might say things the user doesn't want to hear.

The other four are all "presenting yourself" skills — they select, shape, and frame. They're not dishonest, but they're strategic. `interview` is the most explicit about this: it's brag mode. It finds the best version of a story and coaches the user on how to tell it.

This distinction should be preserved in how each skill writes and speaks. Coach talks like a trusted mentor. Interview talks like a prep coach.

---

## Coach takes a time range

`coach` is not a fixed-format output skill — it's a conversation. The user brings a question and a time window:

- "How have I been doing on ownership this month?"
- "Am I getting enough cross-team exposure?"
- "What's been my biggest impact this quarter?"

The skill reads journals in the requested range, then responds to whatever the user is trying to understand. The time range is a parameter, not a separate skill — weekly, monthly, quarterly all use the same skill.

---

## Why interview is separate from coach

Both read journal history. But their goals are opposite:

- **Coach** — "Here's what your journals honestly show."
- **Interview** — "Here's how to frame what you did for an interviewer."

Mixing them would muddy both. A coach that's also trying to make you look good isn't an honest coach. An interview prep tool that leads with gaps isn't useful prep.

---

## Why promote is not just a longer resume

Resume bullets are standalone achievements — each one works without context. A promotion document is a narrative argument: it needs to show growth over time, ownership at the right level, and evidence of the next-level behaviors the company cares about. The audience is also different — a hiring manager scans a resume in 30 seconds; a promotion committee reads a packet and discusses it.

---

## Standup is communication, not reflection

Standup is the only skill with a fixed external audience and a hard time constraint (tomorrow morning). It's also the only skill where "today" (future) matters — the classic format is yesterday / today / blockers. The skill asks one question ("what's your focus today?") to fill that gap. Everything else comes from yesterday's journal entry.

The skill generates from what's in the journal — it does not compensate for thin entries or call out missing context. If the entry is thin, the standup will reflect that.
