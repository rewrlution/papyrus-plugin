# SWE Career Dimensions

> The ground truth reference for all Papyrus skills. Every journal prompt, every coaching output, and every career document generated by Papyrus is grounded in these dimensions.

---

## Why This Document Exists

Major tech companies — Google, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon, Stripe, Dropbox — all evaluate engineers differently on the surface but consistently on the same 7 underlying dimensions. The names change. The rubrics differ. But the substance is the same.

Papyrus journal prompts are designed to capture evidence across these 7 dimensions. When a user asks Papyrus to generate a resume, promotion doc, or interview story, the AI looks for evidence across exactly these dimensions.

---

## The 7 Universal Dimensions

### 1. Scope of Impact

**What it means:** How large is the area you influence? The scope of a software engineer's work expands as they grow — from individual tasks to features, systems, teams, organizations, and eventually industry-wide influence.

**How it progresses:**
- Junior → Single task or bug fix
- Mid → Feature or component
- Senior → Full project or system
- Staff → Multiple teams or org-wide
- Principal/Distinguished → Industry or company-wide

**What to capture in a journal:**
- What systems or areas did your work touch today?
- How many people (users, teammates, other teams) were affected?
- Was your work contained to your team, or did it reach beyond?

**What companies call it:**
- Google: "Scope of leadership"
- Meta: "Project impact"
- Dropbox: "Scope of ownership"
- Stripe: "Scope of influence"
- Amazon: "Breadth and depth"

---

### 2. Ownership & Accountability

**What it means:** The difference between executing a task someone else defined versus being responsible for an outcome end-to-end. Owners define the problem, make the calls, and are on the hook if something goes wrong.

**How it progresses:**
- Junior → Owns individual tasks within a defined project
- Mid → Owns features or components end-to-end
- Senior → Owns full projects; defines scope for others
- Staff → Owns initiative-level outcomes across teams

**What to capture in a journal:**
- Did you drive something from idea to production, or execute a piece?
- Were you the person who would be called if it broke?
- Did you define the problem, or solve a problem handed to you?
- What decisions were yours to make?

**What companies call it:**
- Amazon: "Ownership" (one of their 16 leadership principles)
- Buffer: Core pillar of their entire framework
- Honeycomb: Explicitly separated from scope to avoid confusion
- Meta: Part of "Direction" — what you drive vs. what you do

---

### 3. Technical Execution & System Design

**What it means:** The quality, reliability, and sophistication of the technical work itself. This starts as "writes working code" and matures into "designs systems that scale across the organization for years."

**How it progresses:**
- Junior → Writes correct, reviewable code; learns established patterns
- Mid → Designs components well; handles complexity within a system
- Senior → Designs full systems with appropriate tradeoffs; considers future requirements
- Staff → Shapes architectural direction across systems; technical strategy

**What to capture in a journal:**
- What technical approach did you take, and why?
- What tradeoffs did you evaluate?
- Did you write a design doc, RFC, or architecture proposal?
- What complexity did you reduce or introduce?
- What will still be holding up in 2 years?

**What companies call it:**
- Google: "Technical leadership" (L5→L6 is primarily about this)
- Dropbox: "Domain expertise" and "Innovation"
- Microsoft: "Architectural thinking"
- Amazon: "Technical difficulty" in promotion criteria

---

### 4. Multiplier Effect

**What it means:** Whether your presence makes others faster, better, or more capable. This includes mentoring, code review quality, knowledge sharing, unblocking teammates, building internal tools, and improving processes.

A 1x engineer ships their own work. A 10x engineer makes 10 other engineers ship more and better work.

**How it progresses:**
- Junior → Learns from others; occasionally helps peers
- Mid → Mentors newer engineers; gives useful code reviews; shares knowledge
- Senior → Systematically develops others; unblocks teams; creates leverage through docs/tools
- Staff → Multiplies entire teams; changes how the org works; builds platforms others build on

**What to capture in a journal:**
- Who did you help today, and how?
- Did you unblock someone? How long were they stuck before you helped?
- Did you write docs, create a tool, or improve a process that helps others?
- Did you give a code review that changed someone's approach?
- Who are you actively developing over time?

**What companies call it:**
- Google: "Leadership without title"
- Meta: "Direction" (influencing others' work)
- Dropbox: "Technical Leadership & Mentorship"
- Amazon: "Force multiplier" (Principal+ expectation)
- Stripe: "Elevate ambitions" and being a role model

---

### 5. Delivery Velocity

**What it means:** How consistently and reliably you ship meaningful work. Not just speed — reliability, quality on first attempt, ability to handle ambiguity, and removing blockers rather than creating them.

**How it progresses:**
- Junior → Delivers assigned tasks on schedule with guidance
- Mid → Delivers autonomously; handles ambiguity within a project
- Senior → Drives team delivery; identifies and removes blockers proactively
- Staff → Accelerates org-level delivery; changes how teams work

**What to capture in a journal:**
- Did you deliver what you planned to today?
- What slowed you down — and did you remove that blocker or work around it?
- Where did you hit ambiguity? How did you resolve it?
- What did you do to help your team move faster?

**What companies call it:**
- Dropbox: "Velocity" (one of three core impact principles)
- Square: "Execution"
- Levels.fyi: "Autonomy" and ability to handle "ambiguity"
- Amazon: Measured through concrete outcomes — latency, cost, feature launches

---

### 6. Cross-functional Collaboration

**What it means:** The ability to work effectively with people outside your immediate team — other engineering teams, product managers, designers, data scientists, legal, executives. Includes communication clarity, trust-building, and influence without authority.

**How it progresses:**
- Junior → Collaborates well within their own team
- Mid → Manages cross-team dependencies; communicates across functions
- Senior → Builds trust across teams; influences decisions outside direct control
- Staff → Aligns multiple teams and orgs; shapes cross-org strategy

**What to capture in a journal:**
- Did you interact with anyone outside your team today?
- Did you communicate a complex technical idea to a non-technical person?
- Did you build or maintain trust with another team?
- Did you resolve a conflict or misalignment?
- Did you influence a decision you didn't have formal authority over?

**What companies call it:**
- Meta: "People" dimension in their framework
- Google: Project Oxygen found this matters more than technical skills for senior+
- Dropbox: "Culture" as one of four pillars
- Square: "Team Building" and "Leadership"

---

### 7. Judgment & Decision-making

**What it means:** Making good calls, especially under uncertainty. Knowing when to ship vs. when to wait. Choosing what NOT to build. Balancing short-term delivery with long-term health. Learning visibly from mistakes.

**How it progresses:**
- Junior → Follows established patterns; seeks guidance on tradeoffs
- Mid → Makes decisions within a project; recognizes tradeoffs
- Senior → Makes complex tradeoffs independently; considers 2+ year implications
- Staff → Sets criteria for org-wide tradeoffs; shapes how decisions get made

**What to capture in a journal:**
- What decision did you make today where multiple options existed?
- What did you choose NOT to do — and why?
- What risks did you identify and how did you mitigate them?
- Did you prioritize short-term speed or long-term health? Why?
- If you made a mistake, how did you catch it and correct it?

**What companies call it:**
- Dropbox: "Judgment" in project leadership
- Honeycomb: "Avoiding the scope trap" — knowing when not to build
- Stripe: "Obsessing over users" as the guiding principle for decisions
- Google/Amazon: Smart tradeoffs evaluated in every promotion packet

## The Meta Formula

```
Your level = Scope × Multiplier × Execution
```

- A junior engineer with small scope can still be excellent with high execution and growing multiplier.
- A senior engineer with broad scope but low multiplier stalls — scope alone doesn't justify the level.
- A staff engineer who doesn't execute eventually loses the credibility to set direction.

All three legs must hold.

## How Dimensions Shift by Level

The 7 dimensions apply at every level. What changes is the **expected weight** of each:

| Dimension | Junior | Mid | Senior | Staff |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Scope | Low | Growing | High | Very high |
| Ownership | Task-level | Feature-level | Project-level | Org-level |
| Technical Execution | Core focus | Core focus | Table stakes | Table stakes |
| Multiplier | Minimal | Starting | Core focus | Core focus |
| Delivery | Learning | Solid | Expected | Expected |
| Collaboration | Team only | Cross-team starts | Cross-team | Multi-org |
| Judgment | Guided | Growing | Independent | Strategic |

### Junior engineers
Focus almost entirely on **Technical Execution** and **Delivery**. Scope is intentionally small. Multiplier is minimal — they are net consumers of mentorship, which is expected. The job is to learn fast and deliver reliably.

### Mid-level engineers
**Ownership** becomes central — the shift from "I did the task" to "I drove the feature." **Multiplier** starts to matter: helping juniors, giving real code reviews. Scope expands from tasks to full components.

### Senior engineers
**Multiplier** and **Collaboration** become just as important as execution. Technical Execution is now table stakes — it's expected, not distinguished. The growth edge is in scope expansion and developing others. This is where most engineers plateau if they stay heads-down.

### Staff engineers
**Scope** and **Multiplier** dominate. Execution and Delivery are table stakes. The key question is no longer "what did you build?" but "what did you enable that wouldn't have happened without you?" Staff engineers also split into archetypes (see below).

## Staff Engineer Archetypes

At staff level, engineers specialize. Will Larson's archetypes from staffeng.com are the best-known framework and are referenced (implicitly or explicitly) across many companies:

| Archetype | Core focus | What to journal |
|---|---|---|
| **Tech Lead** | Team delivery and technical quality | Team velocity, technical decisions, removing blockers for the team |
| **Architect** | Long-term technical direction | RFCs, design decisions, 2-3 year implications, cross-system coherence |
| **Solver** | Hardest unsolved problems | What problem nobody else was solving, how you untangled it, who benefited |
| **Right Hand** | Partnering with engineering leadership | High-stakes decisions influenced, org health, strategy execution |

Most staff engineers lean toward one archetype but operate across multiple. Knowing your archetype shapes which dimensions to emphasize in your journal and career documents.

## Implications for Journal Design

### Five anchors, asked every session

Every journal entry captures evidence across the 7 dimensions via 5 consistent anchors:

```
1. WHAT DID YOU DO?           → Scope + Delivery
2. WHAT DID YOU OWN?          → Ownership
3. WHO DID YOU HELP?          → Multiplier
4. WHAT DECISION DID YOU MAKE? → Judgment + Technical
5. HOW DID YOU COLLABORATE?   → Cross-functional
```

### Level shapes the follow-up, not the anchor

The same five questions are asked at every level. What changes is the **expected answer and the follow-up probing**:

**"Who did you help?" at each level:**
- Junior → "Did anyone help you get unblocked? Did you help a peer with something?"
- Mid → "Did you help a junior? Did you unblock a teammate?"
- Senior → "Who did you mentor? Did you shape someone else's technical decision?"
- Staff → "How did you multiply the output of a team — not just one person?"

This approach keeps the skill simple and consistent while the coaching adapts to the user's context.

### Not every anchor needs an answer every day

Some days are heads-down coding days. Some days are all meetings. The skill should feel like a conversation, not a performance review checklist. If "who did you help?" has no answer today, that's fine — log it and move on. Patterns across weeks matter more than any single entry.
